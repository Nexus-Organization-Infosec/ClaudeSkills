# Sick — call-in-sick message

Help the user tell their manager/team they're taking a sick day, in a message that's ready to send and lands well. The whole trick is restraint: **a good sick-day message is short, professional, and doesn't over-explain.** Long, detailed medical stories are unprofessional, over-share, and — counterintuitively — read as *less* credible, not more. So the goal is a clean, respectful note, not a performance.

Keep it honest, too. If the user is genuinely unwell or needs a mental-health day, a simple straight note is the best tool. Don't invent fake symptoms or elaborate cover stories — that's not what this produces.

## What to ask (only what you actually need)

Get the few details that shape the message, then write it — don't interrogate:
- **Channel & formality** — email, Slack/Teams, or text? Formal workplace or casual team?
- **Who** — manager's name / the team.
- **Scope** — full day, part of the day, or unsure yet / possibly multi-day?
- **Coverage** — any urgent thing today that needs handing off or flagging?
- **Reachability** — will they be reachable for emergencies, or fully off?

If they don't want to answer all of that, make reasonable defaults and go.

## What a good message contains

1. **The point, up front** — you're unwell and taking a sick day today.
2. **Availability** — whether you're reachable for anything urgent, or offline to rest.
3. **Handover** — one line on anything time-sensitive (who's covering, or that X will wait until you're back).
4. **Return** — expected back tomorrow / will update if that changes.
5. **Brief courtesy close.**

## What to leave out

- Graphic or detailed symptoms — "not feeling well" / "under the weather" / "came down with something" is plenty; no one needs the details, and piling them on undercuts credibility.
- Over-apologizing or long justifications — one clean note, not a plea.
- Promises you can't keep ("I'll still finish everything tonight").

## Jurisdiction note

Factor in where the user works, because sick-leave rules differ. In **Germany** (the user's location), an employer can require an *Arbeitsunfähigkeitsbescheinigung* (AU / doctor's sick note) — often from the **3rd** day of illness, but many contracts require it from **day 1** — and you notify your employer *without needing to state the illness*. So: if this looks like it'll run past a day or two, remind them they may need a doctor's note, and reassure that not naming the specific illness is normal and legally fine there. Adjust for another country if they say where they are.

## Output

Give **one ready-to-send message** matched to the channel and tone (offer a second, slightly different, if they'd like options). Keep formal emails to a short paragraph with a subject line; keep Slack/text to a sentence or two.

**Example (Slack, casual team):**
> Morning [name] — I'm not feeling well today so I'm going to take a sick day and rest up. I'll be offline, but reachable by text if anything's urgent. [Coworker] can cover the [X] check-in; everything else can wait till I'm back tomorrow. Thanks!

**Example (email, formal):**
> Subject: Sick day — [your name]
>
> Hi [manager], I woke up unwell this morning and won't be able to work today. I'll be resting and mostly offline; for anything urgent, [coworker] is across the [project]. I expect to be back tomorrow and will let you know if that changes. Thanks for understanding.
